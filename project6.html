Product design case study

From lab values to safe, actionable health interpretation

A responsive health report that turns clinical data into structured, understandable and actionable information.

My role

Lead product designer - member experience and interaction model

Primary users

Members reviewing completed laboratory reports

Core challenge

Explain clinical meaning without diagnosing, alarming or falsely reassuring

  •   Project Name

    Smart Report

  •   Contribution:

    Product strategy, information architecture, interaction design, prototyping and clinical/product alignment

  •   Timeline:

    2026 - Phase 1

  •   Deliverable:

    Mobile-first member experience

  •   Client Name:

    Connect and Heal

The product shift

The legacy report described results. The new experience interprets them.

The previous Smart Report digitised a conventional lab report: it displayed each parameter's status, added educational content and ended with a generic consultation action. It answered “What are my values?” but left members to work out what those values meant together and what to do next.

Phase 1 introduces an interpretation layer on top of finalised laboratory data. The experience must make abnormalities visible, preserve positive context and connect every finding to evidence and an appropriate next step - without presenting itself as a diagnosis.

01 · Overview

Lab Report

Answers “What is in range and what is out?” Biomarkers are split into clear lists with values, reference ranges, profile tags and movement from the previous reading.

02 · Interpretation

Smart Report

Answers “What does this mean and what can I do?” Findings are prioritised into attention, watch and good states, with profile and biomarker drill-downs.

Design principles derived from the risk

Abnormalities lead

Critical and high-severity findings appear first. Positive results remain visible but never compete with urgent information.

Evidence stays traceable

A member can move from a pattern to its contributing biomarkers and trends, then return without losing context.

Confidence is explicit

Insufficient or limited coverage is communicated instead of turning incomplete data into a confident score.

Interpretation is not diagnosis

Clinical patterns come from deterministic rules. Generated content explains those outputs but cannot create new clinical claims.

Information architecture

The experience has three connected levels. Each level answers a different question while allowing members to trace information in both directions.

  1. Report levelLab Report for biomarker status; Smart Report for prioritised interpretation.
  2. Health profile levelA clinical area such as Heart Health or Blood Sugar, with a health score, tier, coverage, pattern insight and parameters.
  3. Biomarker levelValue versus range, historical trend, plain-language meaning, recommendations and appropriate next actions.
A deliberate omission: the engine computes an overall score, but the member experience never displays it. A single number would flatten clinically different concerns and could create false reassurance.

The 2026 design workspace

The Figma work covers reusable components, three mobile entry points, the Lab Report overview, Smart Report interpretation, profile and biomarker detail, partial-data states and responsive web explorations.

Decision 1: separate status from interpretation

Putting every value, pattern and recommendation on one long page would increase cognitive load and make critical information harder to locate. I separated the experience into two tabs with distinct jobs. The report opens on Lab Report so members first see the factual biomarker picture; Smart Report then organises meaning and action.

  • Lab Report: total tested, out-of-range and within-range counts; All versus Key parameter filter; movement where history exists; PDF download and parameter summaries.
  • Smart Report: What Needs Attention, Worth Watching, What's Good and Health Profile Summary.

Decision 2: make severity scannable without creating alarm

What Needs Attention combines different types of evidence, so findings are bucketed into seven understandable categories: critical flags, patterns, elevated markers, deficiencies, imaging, vital signs and screening findings. Severity determines order, while the category explains what kind of evidence the member is seeing.

Long sections show three items by default so members can reach the Health Profile Summary. Critical findings are the exception: they are pinned at the top, never collapsed, never shown in a celebratory treatment and always expose a care pathway.

Decision 3: prevent duplicate and misleading findings

A biomarker that contributes to a detected multi-marker pattern appears inside that pattern instead of being repeated as a separate elevation or deficiency. This engine-driven de-duplication reduces noise and prevents the same concern from feeling more severe simply because it appears multiple times.

Every scored marker has one destination: Moderate or worse goes to What Needs Attention, Low goes to Worth Watching, and No Risk contributes to What's Good. Protective patterns influence the score but do not appear as standalone good news that might offset a concurrent concern.

Decision 4: design for uncertainty and incomplete data

Below approximately 30% coverageShow Insufficient Data and suppress the tier.
30-59% coverageShow the tier with “X of Y parameters tested.”
No previous resultShow a range bar only; do not imply a trend.
Partial lab orderDo not generate a Smart Report until every ordered panel is resulted or closed.
Refused or conflicted valueShow it informationally and never score it.

Actions are matched to the finding

The report is designed to move from understanding to an appropriate next step. Depending on the finding, members can consult a doctor, book a recommended test, monitor a profile, begin a 90-day action plan or follow a matched condition-management recommendation. Critical pathways cannot be disabled by client configuration.

Validation contract

Because this is a safety-sensitive product, design quality is defined by testable behaviour rather than visual completion alone.

  • The report opens on Lab Report and switches tabs without re-fetching scores.
  • In-range and out-of-range counts match their lists.
  • No overall health score appears on any member-facing surface.
  • Critical findings are pinned, actionable and never celebratory.
  • Every finding routes to exactly one correct profile or biomarker destination.
  • Claims in “What This Means” trace to a measured value or fired rule.
  • Back navigation preserves the active tab and previous scroll position.
  • Trends show real readings as continuous lines and never project future values.

What success means

Phase 1 success is not “more time spent reading a report.” It is members finding the right level of information, understanding what requires attention and reaching a safe next step without losing the evidence behind a recommendation.

Report open and entry sourceTab switchingFinding and profile drill-downTrend engagementCare-action conversionCritical-alert surfacing audit

Outcome and evidence

Phase 1 defines the member-facing experience and its clinical-safety contract across entry, overview, interpretation, profile and biomarker flows. Production outcome data is not yet included in the available PRD.

What I would measure next

Comprehension of severity and next steps, successful finding-to-evidence tracing, critical-path visibility, report-to-care conversion, and errors caused by misunderstood confidence or trend states.
